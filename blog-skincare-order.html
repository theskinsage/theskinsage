The correct order to apply your skincare and exactly why it matters

Layering products in the wrong sequence can block actives, break down formulas and cause your skin to react. Here is the clinical explanation — and the sequence that actually works.

The Skin Sage · © 2025 · Educational and informational guidance

There is a reason skincare application order is one of the most searched questions about skincare — and one of the worst answered. Most advice online is either too simplified to be useful or contradictory depending on which source you ask.

Here is the honest clinical explanation. No brand agenda. No product recommendations. Just the reasoning behind the sequence so you understand why — not just what.

"Understanding why the order matters is more useful than memorising a list. The principle stays with you. The list gets forgotten."

The underlying principle

Skincare products are formulated to work at specific depths of the skin. Lightweight water-based products contain smaller molecules designed to penetrate deeper. Heavier oil-based products and occlusives are designed to work at the surface — sealing in what has been applied beneath them.

If you apply a heavy cream before a water-based serum, you have created a physical barrier. The serum cannot penetrate. It sits on top of the cream, does nothing for your skin and you wonder why it is not working. The product is fine. The order is wrong.

The correct morning sequence

Morning routine — correct order
01
CleanseA gentle cleanser. Morning cleansing removes overnight product residue and prepares the skin to receive what follows. Do not skip this even if you cleansed the night before.
02
Tone — if usingApplied to damp or dry skin depending on formulation. Toners prepare the skin's pH and can provide a first layer of hydration or treatment depending on the formula.
03
Treatment serums — thinnest firstVitamin C serum typically goes here in the morning. Apply the thinnest consistency first. If using multiple serums, allow each one to absorb before applying the next.
04
Eye creamApplied before moisturiser. The eye area skin is thinner and the eye cream should reach it before a heavier moisturiser creates a barrier over it.
05
MoisturiserSeals in the treatment products applied beneath and provides hydration to the barrier. Choose a weight appropriate to your skin type.
06
SPF — always last in the morningThis is critical and one of the most commonly misapplied products. SPF must be the final step before makeup. Applying anything over your SPF dilutes it and reduces its protection factor. Every morning. Without exception.

The correct evening sequence

Evening routine — correct order
01
Double cleanse if wearing SPF or makeupOil cleanser or micellar water first to remove SPF and makeup. Followed by your regular cleanser. Single cleansing over SPF leaves residue that blocks everything applied after it.
02
Tone — if usingSame principle as morning.
03
Treatment serums and activesEvening is the time for stronger actives — retinol, AHAs, BHAs — because they are photosensitive or because your skin is in repair mode overnight. Apply thinnest to thickest.
04
Eye creamSame principle as morning.
05
MoisturiserCan be richer in the evening. Your skin is in repair mode overnight and benefits from more occlusive support.
06
Facial oil — if usingOil goes last in the evening. It is the most occlusive step and should seal in everything beneath it. Never apply oil before a water-based product — water will not absorb through oil.

The most common order mistakes

SPF applied before moisturiser. Extremely common. SPF must be the absolute final step in your morning routine. Applying moisturiser over SPF dilutes the sun protection factor. You are not getting the protection level the bottle states.

Facial oil applied before serum. Oil creates an occlusive barrier. Nothing applied after it will penetrate properly. Oil always goes last unless it is a dry oil used as a carrier — and even then the principles apply.

Vitamin C applied after moisturiser. Vitamin C is a water-soluble antioxidant that needs to reach the deeper layers of the epidermis. Apply it before your moisturiser so it can penetrate before being sealed in.

Retinol and AHA used on the same evening without care. Both are exfoliating and when combined incorrectly can cause significant irritation. If you use both, consider alternating evenings or applying retinol first and waiting before applying AHA.

The simplest rule to remember: Thinnest consistency first. Most occlusive last. SPF always final in the morning. And if you are unsure whether a product goes before or after another — apply the one with the more active ingredients first.

When to apply to damp versus dry skin

This makes a significant difference for certain products. Hyaluronic acid applied to completely dry skin can actually draw moisture from the deeper layers of the skin if the environment is dry. Apply it to slightly damp skin after cleansing or toning to lock in existing surface moisture.

Retinol is typically recommended on dry skin. Applying retinol to damp skin increases penetration which can increase irritation. If you are new to retinol or have sensitive skin, applying to dry skin reduces the risk of reaction.

Most other serums work well on either damp or dry skin — follow the product instructions where they exist.
