Why your skincare is not working — and it is not the products fault

Most people are using the right ingredients in the wrong order, at the wrong time, on unprepared skin. Before you buy anything new — read this.

The Skin Sage · © 2025 · Educational and informational guidance

If your skincare routine is not giving you results, the most common reaction is to buy something new. A different serum. A stronger active. Something you saw on TikTok that someone else swore changed their skin.

But here is what most people do not know — and what the industry has absolutely no incentive to tell you. The problem is almost never the products. It is almost always the routine.

"Your skin is not failing you. Your routine is failing your skin. There is a significant difference."

The order you apply your products matters enormously

Skincare products are formulated to be applied in a specific sequence. Apply them out of order and you can physically prevent active ingredients from penetrating the skin, neutralise ingredients that react poorly when combined in the wrong sequence, and create a barrier on the skin's surface that blocks everything you apply after it.

The general principle is thin to thick — water-based products before oil-based products, lighter textures before heavier ones. But there are important exceptions and nuances that most tutorials gloss over entirely.

The correct general sequence: Cleanse → Tone (if using) → Treatment serums (thinnest first) → Eye cream → Moisturiser → SPF (morning only, always last)

Where most people go wrong is applying their SPF before their moisturiser, using their facial oil before their water-based serum, or applying vitamin C after their moisturiser where it cannot penetrate.

Applying actives to an unprepared barrier

An active ingredient — retinol, vitamin C, niacinamide, AHAs, BHAs — can only work if it can actually reach the layer of skin it is targeting. If your barrier is compromised, dehydrated or congested, actives may sit on the surface, cause irritation, or absorb unevenly.

This is why so many people find that vitamin C serum tingles uncomfortably, or that retinol causes flaking even at a low concentration. The issue is not usually the product. It is the skin it is landing on.

"Before asking whether you need a stronger active — ask whether your skin is in a state to receive it."

Preparing the skin means ensuring adequate hydration, a functioning barrier and a clean surface. That sequence — cleanse properly, hydrate, then treat — sounds obvious but most people skip directly to their actives on skin that is not ready for them.

Ingredient conflicts nobody warned you about

Some ingredients interact poorly when layered directly on top of each other. The most common ones people encounter are vitamin C combined with certain AHAs at low pH, retinol combined with benzoyl peroxide, and physical SPF combined with vitamin C which can cause oxidation and actually increase free radical damage rather than protect against it.

This does not mean you cannot use these ingredients — it means the timing and layering matters. Some of these combinations work perfectly well when applied at different times of day. Others need a buffer product between them. Others are simply best kept on separate days.

Before adding anything new to your routine — audit what you already have. Two products doing the same job. An active applied in the wrong order. An ingredient conflict you did not know existed. These are almost always more impactful than buying something new.

The real reason results take so long

Skin cell turnover takes approximately 28 days in younger skin and slows progressively with age. This means visible results from most skincare changes take a minimum of four to six weeks to appear — and significant changes to concerns like pigmentation or texture can take three to six months of consistent correct use.

Most people abandon a routine after two to three weeks because they have not seen results. In reality, a product that is being used correctly and consistently simply may not have had enough time to work. Or the routine has been disrupted by adding something new, changing the order, or using products inconsistently.

The most effective skincare routines are often the simplest ones. Consistently applied. In the correct order. On prepared skin. Given adequate time.

What to do before you buy anything else

Write down every product you use. Morning and evening. In the order you use them. Look at each one and ask — do I know what this is doing? Am I applying it correctly? Is it suited to my skin type? Is there anything in my routine that might be working against it?

Most people find that this exercise alone identifies two or three things that are either wrong, unnecessary or counterproductive. Fixing those things costs nothing and often delivers better results than any new purchase.
