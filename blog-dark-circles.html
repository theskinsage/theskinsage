What your dark circles are actually telling you — a clinical explanation

Dark circles are the most searched skin concern in the UK. Most advice misses the real causes entirely. Vascular, pigmented and structural — here is what your under-eyes are actually saying.

The Skin Sage · © 2025 · Educational and informational guidance

Dark circles are one of the most misunderstood skin concerns because they are treated as a single problem with a single solution. The reality is that dark circles have several distinct causes — and the treatment that works for one type can be completely ineffective or even counterproductive for another.

Understanding which type you have is the first step. Most eye creams do not tell you this. Most advice online does not tell you this. We will.

"Treating vascular dark circles with brightening ingredients is like treating a headache with a plaster. You are addressing the wrong problem entirely."

The three primary types of dark circles

Type One — Vascular

What you see: A blue, purple or reddish-purple tone under the eyes. Often worse in the morning and after alcohol, poor sleep or high screen time. May improve when you press gently on the skin.

What is causing it: Blood pooling in the fine capillaries beneath the extremely thin periorbital skin. The skin under the eyes is some of the thinnest on the body — as little as 0.5mm compared to 2mm elsewhere on the face — which makes the blood vessels beneath it visible as a coloured shadow.

What actually helps: Improving circulation and lymphatic drainage. Reducing inflammation. Caffeine-based eye creams have some evidence for temporary constriction of blood vessels. Lymphatic drainage technique targeting the periorbital and cervical nodes. Addressing the underlying causes — sleep, hydration, screen time, alcohol intake.

Type Two — Pigmented

What you see: A brown or dark brown tone. More common in Fitzpatrick skin types III to VI. Does not significantly change with sleep or time of day. The discolouration is in the skin itself rather than beneath it.

What is causing it: Melanin deposits in the periorbital skin. Often triggered by friction — rubbing the eyes, removing eye makeup without adequate lubrication, allergic reactions that cause itching. Sun exposure in an area that is difficult to adequately protect with SPF. Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation from previous skin irritation.

What actually helps: Consistent SPF specifically around the eye area. Addressing the friction source — careful makeup removal, treating allergies. Ingredients with evidence for pigmentation — vitamin C, niacinamide, kojic acid — used very carefully around the eye area given the sensitivity of the skin. Patience. Pigmented dark circles respond slowly.

Type Three — Structural

What you see: Shadowing rather than actual discolouration. The under-eye area appears darker because of a hollow or depression — the tear trough — that casts a shadow. Often accompanied by the appearance of puffiness above the hollow.

What is causing it: Loss of volume in the periorbital fat pads that occurs with age. The tear trough — the groove between the lower eyelid and the cheek — becomes more pronounced as the fat pad beneath it depletes. This creates a shadow that reads as dark circles but is actually a structural change in facial anatomy.

What actually helps: Skincare has limited impact on structural dark circles. Addressing dehydration and inflammation can help reduce the depth of the hollow but cannot replace volume. For significant structural dark circles, the most effective interventions are aesthetic — hyaluronic acid filler placed by a qualified practitioner into the tear trough. This falls outside the scope of skincare coaching and requires consultation with a qualified aesthetic practitioner.

Most people have a combination

The majority of people with significant dark circles have a combination of two or three types simultaneously. A vascular component made worse by sleep deprivation. A pigmented component made worse by eye rubbing. A structural component that has become more pronounced with age. Each element requires a different approach.

This is why generic eye cream advice is so ineffective. An eye cream formulated for vascular dark circles will not touch a pigmented component. And no eye cream in existence will correct a structural hollow.

"Before spending money on an eye cream — identify which type of dark circle you have. The right intervention for the wrong type is money wasted."

The role of lymphatic drainage

For vascular dark circles specifically, manual lymphatic drainage is one of the most effective interventions available — and one of the most overlooked. The lymphatic system is responsible for removing fluid, waste products and inflammatory mediators from tissue. When lymphatic flow is sluggish, fluid pools in the periorbital area contributing to both puffiness and the appearance of vascular dark circles.

The correct technique begins at the terminus — the lymph node at the collarbone — to open the drainage pathway before working toward the face. Draining the neck nodes before the face nodes, and always directing fluid downward and outward away from the periorbital area.

This is why we include a clinically correct lymphatic self massage technique in our Decoded and Unlocked consultations. It is the most powerful free tool for periorbital health — and almost no skincare advice mentions it.

What the under-eye area reflects systemically

Dark circles — particularly vascular dark circles — are one of the most reliable external indicators of internal state. Poor sleep is visible under the eyes within 24 hours. Alcohol causes peripheral vasodilation and fluid retention that shows under the eyes within hours. Dehydration reduces skin plumpness making vessels more visible. High cortisol from chronic stress increases inflammation and impairs lymphatic function.

This does not mean dark circles are a moral failing or something to feel embarrassed about. It means that the most effective approach addresses what is driving them — not just what they look like.

A simple self-test for vascular vs pigmented dark circles: Press gently on the dark area with your finger for a few seconds then release. If the darkness temporarily lightens when you press — it is likely vascular. If there is no change — it is likely pigmented. Many people see a partial response — a combination of both types.
